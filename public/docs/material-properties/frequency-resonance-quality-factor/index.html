    Frequency, Resonance, and Quality Factor #

    Understanding the relationship between frequency, resonance, and quality factor is fundamental to impulse excitation testing and material characterization.

    Natural Frequencies #

    Definition #

    Natural frequencies are the frequencies at which a system oscillates when disturbed from equilibrium. For impulse excitation testing, these frequencies are directly related to material properties.

    Physical Significance #

    Material properties:

    • Elastic moduli
    • Density
    • Geometry
    • Boundary conditions

    System characteristics:

    • Stiffness
    • Mass
    • Damping
    • Geometry

    Frequency Calculation #

    Flexural frequency:

    f_f = (k_f / 2π) * √(E / ρ) * (t / L²)

    Torsional frequency:

    f_t = (k_t / 2π) * √(G / ρ) * (1 / L)

    Where:

    • f_f, f_t = natural frequencies
    • k_f, k_t = frequency factors
    • E, G = elastic moduli
    • ρ = density
    • t, L = dimensions

    Resonance Behavior #

    Resonance Definition #

    Resonance occurs when the excitation frequency matches a natural frequency, resulting in maximum vibration amplitude.

    Resonance Characteristics #

    Amplitude response:

    • Maximum at resonance
    • Decreases away from resonance
    • Frequency dependent
    • Damping dependent

    Phase response:

    • 0° at resonance
    • Changes with frequency
    • Damping dependent
    • Material dependent

    Resonance Measurement #

    Frequency sweep:

    • Vary excitation frequency
    • Measure response amplitude
    • Identify resonance peaks
    • Calculate natural frequencies

    Impulse excitation:

    • Apply broad-band impulse
    • Measure frequency response
    • Identify natural frequencies
    • Calculate material properties

    Quality Factor #

    Definition #

    The quality factor (Q) is a measure of energy dissipation in a vibrating system:

    Q = 2π × (Stored Energy / Energy Lost per Cycle)

    Physical Significance #

    Energy dissipation:

    • Higher Q = lower damping
    • Lower Q = higher damping
    • Material characteristic
    • Process dependent

    Resonance sharpness:

    • Higher Q = sharper peaks
    • Lower Q = broader peaks
    • Measurement accuracy
    • Material behavior

    Q-Factor Calculation #

    From resonance width:

    Q = f₀ / Δf

    Where:

    • f₀ = resonance frequency
    • Δf = half-power bandwidth

    From free vibration decay:

    Q = π / δ

    Where:

    • δ = logarithmic decrement

    Frequency Factors #

    Flexural Frequency Factors #

    Rectangular bars:

    • First mode: k_f = 1.506
    • Second mode: k_f = 2.500
    • Higher modes: k_f increases

    Cylindrical bars:

    • First mode: k_f = 1.571
    • Second mode: k_f = 2.500
    • Higher modes: k_f increases

    Torsional Frequency Factors #

    Rectangular bars:

    • First mode: k_t = 1.000
    • Second mode: k_t = 2.000
    • Higher modes: k_t increases

    Cylindrical bars:

    • First mode: k_t = 1.000
    • Second mode: k_t = 2.000
    • Higher modes: k_t increases

    Mode Shapes #

    Flexural Modes #

    First mode:

    • Single half-wave
    • Maximum at center
    • Zero at supports
    • Primary mode

    Higher modes:

    • Multiple half-waves
    • Multiple nodes
    • Higher frequencies
    • More complex shapes

    Torsional Modes #

    First mode:

    • Single twist
    • Maximum at edges
    • Zero at center
    • Primary mode

    Higher modes:

    • Multiple twists
    • Multiple nodes
    • Higher frequencies
    • More complex shapes
