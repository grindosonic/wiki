    Fundamentals of Damping #

    Damping is a crucial material property that describes how materials dissipate energy during vibration. Understanding damping is essential for impulse excitation testing and material characterization.

    What is Damping? #

    Definition #

    Damping is the process by which vibrational energy is dissipated in a material, converting mechanical energy into heat. It’s a fundamental property that affects material behavior and performance.

    Physical Mechanisms #

    Material damping:

    • Internal friction
    • Viscous effects
    • Hysteresis
    • Molecular motion

    Structural damping:

    • Joint friction
    • Support damping
    • Air resistance
    • External losses

    Damping Parameters #

    Quality Factor (Q) #

    Definition: The quality factor is a measure of energy dissipation, defined as:

    Q = 2π × (Stored Energy / Energy Lost per Cycle)

    Physical significance:

    • Higher Q = lower damping
    • Lower Q = higher damping
    • Material characteristic
    • Process dependent

    Loss Factor (η) #

    Definition: The loss factor is an alternative damping measure:

    η = 1 / Q

    Physical significance:

    • Higher η = higher damping
    • Lower η = lower damping
    • Material characteristic
    • Process dependent

    Damping Ratio (ζ) #

    Definition: The damping ratio is:

    ζ = η / 2

    Physical significance:

    • Critical damping occurs at ζ = 1
    • Underdamped: ζ < 1
    • Overdamped: ζ > 1

    Material Damping #

    Metals #

    Damping mechanisms:

    • Dislocation motion
    • Grain boundary sliding
    • Phase transformations
    • Magnetic effects

    Typical values:

    • Steel: Q = 100-1000
    • Aluminum: Q = 200-2000
    • Titanium: Q = 50-500
    • Copper: Q = 100-1000

    Ceramics #

    Damping mechanisms:

    • Grain boundary sliding
    • Pore effects
    • Phase transformations
    • Microcracking

    Typical values:

    • Alumina: Q = 1000-10000
    • Silicon carbide: Q = 500-5000
    • Zirconia: Q = 100-1000
    • Glass: Q = 1000-10000

    Polymers #

    Damping mechanisms:

    • Molecular motion
    • Chain relaxation
    • Glass transition
    • Viscoelastic effects

    Typical values:

    • Thermoplastics: Q = 10-100
    • Thermosets: Q = 50-500
    • Elastomers: Q = 1-10
    • Composites: Q = 20-200

    Composites #

    Damping mechanisms:

    • Matrix damping
    • Fiber damping
    • Interface damping
    • Delamination

    Typical values:

    • Carbon fiber: Q = 100-1000
    • Glass fiber: Q = 50-500
    • Aramid fiber: Q = 20-200
    • Metal matrix: Q = 100-1000
